I Was Doom Scrolling at the Markets and Accidentally Built a Product

A Sunday afternoon, a car park, and the idea that your own thoughts might be worth more than everyone else's.

Watch this first — 3 minutes
Harvard professor Arthur Brooks explains why boredom isn't the enemy. It's the default mode network switching on — the part of your brain that finds meaning. It's exactly what happened to me at the markets.

▶ You Need to Be Bored — Arthur Brooks, Harvard Business Review →

Sunday afternoon. My wife disappears into an Asian food shop at the markets. I'm standing outside, phone in hand, doing what I always do when I have two minutes to myself.

Doom scrolling.

You know the feeling. Something flashes past. You half laugh. Then it's gone. Five minutes later you couldn't tell anyone what you just saw. But you keep going anyway. The thumb just keeps moving.

And then something made me stop.

I caught myself mid-scroll and thought — why am I actually doing this?

Not in a judgmental way. Just genuinely curious. What am I getting from this? The honest answer: almost nothing. A mild hit of something. Entertainment that evaporates the second it arrives. I wasn't learning anything. I wasn't connecting with anyone. I was just consuming. Other people's highlights. Other people's outrage. Other people's thoughts.

So I closed Instagram and opened Missy instead.

Missy is my AI thinking partner. I talk to her the way some people talk to a mate — out loud, half-formed, just working things through. And something shifted the moment I did.

Brooks talks about the default mode network — the part of your brain that switches on when you stop being occupied. That's what switched on for me. Not because I put my phone down — but because I changed what I was doing with it.

The Thing Nobody Says About Social Media

Within about ten minutes of just talking, I landed on something that felt true.

Social media is built on one quiet lie — that other people's thoughts are more interesting than your own. They're not. You just never had anywhere to keep yours.

And it's not accidental. The whole business model depends on you not feeling quite enough on your own. Keep you slightly insecure. Keep you comparing. Keep you scrolling. That's not a side effect — that's the product.

It's peer group pressure at scale. The same mechanism as workplace bullying, just pointed at millions of people simultaneously.

And here's what really got me — when you DO leave something of yourself on social media — a comment, a reaction, a thought that someone else's post triggered in you — it lives on their property. Forever. Buried in their feed. You'll never find it again.

Then My 14 Year Old Walked In

I'm sitting there thinking all this through, and my son walks into the room. Doesn't say hello. Doesn't ask what I'm doing. Just says:

"Quantity over quality."

Then walks out. No context. No explanation. Just dropped it and left.

Kid accidentally summarised everything wrong with social media in four words and didn't even know it.

The AI Irony Nobody's Talking About

Here's the thing that hit me next, and it's uncomfortable.

AI was built by consuming everyone's content. Your photos. Your words. Your ideas. Scraped from the internet without asking. Fed into systems that learned from your creativity and gave you nothing back.

Then that same AI floods the same platforms with more content — built on what it took from you in the first place. It's a giant loop. The individual gets nothing. The platforms get everything.

AI can improve your life — not through getting recognised, but through finding yourself. Because when you find yourself, you have better thoughts. You become more productive naturally. The recognition follows on its own.

What If Your Feed Was Yours?

So I'm sitting in the car park waiting for my wife — she's moved from the Asian food shop to somewhere else now — and this idea starts forming.

What if instead of scrolling other people's content, you had a feed of your own thinking?

Not a journal. Not a note-taking app. A feed. Vertical scroll. Cards. The same frictionless mechanic as doom scrolling — but pointing inward instead of outward.

Every insight you have. Every blog idea from 2am. Every comment you left on someone else's post — now a card in your own feed. And when a new thought hits, you tap + and a new card appears. One tap. Done.

The cards don't die. They grow.

We're calling it HomeFeed.

Your Personal HomeFeed.

Brooks says boredom is where meaning lives. I think he's right. But he doesn't say what to do when the big thoughts surface. HomeFeed is the answer to that gap. Boredom surfaces the question. HomeFeed captures the answer.

Ideas Need Time to Breathe

Social media demands you react instantly. Something happens — politically, personally, professionally — and everyone posts within minutes. Half formed. Emotional. Then you regret it.

HomeFeed flips the timing. You have a strong opinion today. It goes into your private feed as a card. You sit with it. Add to it three days later. See it from a different angle a week on. Then — when it's actually ready — you push it out.

Not a hot take. A considered position.

HomeFeed is the incubator between your first thought and your best one.

My Stepson and the AFL Finals

My stepson spends hours drawing up the AFL finals on paper. Predicting who'll win the flag. Working through the logic of the whole season. He called it correctly once — years ago.

And nobody captured it.

What if that was his first HomeFeed card? Not AI. Not tech. Just: photo of his finals on paper, date, his prediction. At the end of the season, that card surfaces back. He was right. That moment is his. Forever.

That's self worth built from the inside. Not from likes.

Australia just banned social media for under 16s. Good call. But banning something doesn't fill the gap. You need something better to point kids toward — not another platform, just somewhere to keep their own thinking.

Social Media Comes to You. HomeFeed Waits for You.

Here's the design principle I want to lock in early — because this is where every good idea goes wrong.

The second you add notifications, suggested content, engagement metrics — it becomes the thing you were running away from.

HomeFeed never comes to you. You go to it.

The only things that come to you are from people. Intentionally. One card at a time. Your stepson shares his AFL finals to the family feed. Someone whose thinking genuinely connects with yours surfaces one card — not their whole feed, just one thought.

Just people, occasionally, deliberately sharing a thought worth sharing.

Self Knowledge is the Protection

A person who knows their own thinking is much harder to manipulate. By social media. By peer pressure. By workplace bullying. By politicians who say "speak up" while pointing you toward platforms designed for outrage, not thinking.

Brooks says get better at boredom and watch your life change. I think he's onto something even bigger — get better at capturing what boredom surfaces, and watch your thinking change.

Doom scrolling becomes mining.

Your scattered thoughts come home.

The feed builds itself just from how you already live.

I figured all of this out in a car park on a Sunday afternoon. Bored. Waiting. Doing nothing important.

Turns out changing what you look at on your phone changes how you think.

Every other platform was built on your words.
This one gives them back to you.
